01 — Overview

The Design Problem

Citizens in India have a limited mechanism for grievance redress, especially for day-to-day issues. Most of these mechanisms are opaque with diffused accountability, as there is no reliable mechanism to file, track, monitor, escalate, and audit grievances. My role was to co-design a user-centred service from the ground up: mapping user journeys across six filing touchpoints, producing a service blueprint that made the full system legible, specifying interaction design for officer-facing digital forms, and building a performance measurement framework to make district accountability visible.

The Citizen Journey

No way to know if a complaint was received, who was responsible, or what the deadline was. No mechanism to escalate if deadlines were missed without physically visiting an office.

The Officer Experience

No structured format for recording how a complaint was investigated or what action was taken. Officers could close complaints without evidence of resolution.

The System Problem

No public record of performance. No pressure on districts or departments to improve. Senior officials had no reliable data on where the system was failing.

02 — Service Blueprint

Mapping the end-to-end service: six touchpoints, three escalation tiers, one integrated system.

The service blueprint maps the full citizen journey from first contact to resolution, showing both what the citizen experiences (front stage) and what happens invisibly behind it (back stage). The first design decision was how citizens would enter the service. Rather than a single channel, the blueprint shows six parallel touchpoints, each serving a different user journey and context. All six are treated as equivalent entry points. The second non-negotiable design decision was automatic escalation. Any service that requires a citizen to initiate an appeal has already failed them.

Six User Journey Entry Points
  1. Bapuji Seva Kendra (BSK) — Physical facilitation centres for assisted filing, designed for citizens without digital access or mobility.
  2. Phone Helpline — Voice-based filing for citizens who cannot travel or write.
  3. Mobile Application and Website — Self-filing for digitally enabled citizens.
  4. Grievance Redress Officer (GRO) — Direct in-person filing with the responsible officer.
  5. Department Walk-In — Filing at the relevant department for citizens who know which agency is responsible.
  6. Social Audit Gram Sabha — Collective filing through public hearings for community-level grievances.
Three Escalation Tiers
Tier 1 — 30 Days
Grievance Redress Officer (GRO)
One level above the responsible delivery officer. Site visit and meeting with complainant required. If Action Taken Report not filed within 30 days, automatic escalation triggers — no action required from the citizen.
Tier 2 — 30 Days
District Grievance Redress Officer
First appellate authority. Independent call centre quality check after resolution. If citizen reports unsatisfied, auto-escalation to Tier 3.
Tier 3 — 30 Days
State Grievance Redress Cell
Final escalation. Social Audit Unit can trigger parallel review through published audit reports.
The service blueprint made one thing visible that no one had named before: every point at which the system required the citizen to take action was a point at which the citizen could fall out of the service entirely. The design response was to remove as many of those points as possible and automate the rest.
03 — ATR Design

Designing the Officer-Facing Interface

The Action Taken Report (ATR) is the digital form an investigating officer files to document how a complaint was investigated and resolved. In the existing system it was a blank paper form — no field-level logic, no mandatory evidence, no connection to the officer’s digital identity. The redesign treated the ATR as a user-centred interface design problem with two users: the officer filing it, and the citizen and senior officials who would rely on what it contained. Five Interaction Design Decisions

User Need Identified
Officers needed a format that made evidence mandatory, not optional. Senior officials needed to verify that investigations actually occurred.
Design Decision
Dual signatures made mandatory on the Final Report: both investigating officer and reporting officer must sign. Photographs from site visits auto-populate into the final report and cannot be removed before submission.
Rationale
Reporting officers were frequently unaware of how enquiries were conducted, enabling easy manipulation. Dual signatures created shared accountability. Photo auto-population closed the space between filing and evidence.
User Need Identified
Citizens needed proof of filing and a way to identify who was responsible and by when — without having to ask.
Design Decision
Mandatory acknowledgement receipt at the point of filing containing: unique complaint ID, official designation and contact of the assigned GRO, deadline for resolution, and instructions for filing a first appeal.
Rationale
The receipt is the citizen’s primary instrument in the service journey. Every field was designed around a specific moment of uncertainty: Was it received? Who has it? By when? What if nothing happens?
User Need Identified
Citizens needed resolution to be verified by someone other than the officer who resolved the complaint.
Design Decision
Mandatory quality check call from an independent call centre after every ATR submission. If the citizen reports unsatisfied, the system auto-escalates rather than closing.
Rationale
Self-reported resolution data is worthless when the assessor is the same person who resolved the complaint. Independence of the quality check is what makes the data credible enough to act on.
User Need Identified
Officers needed a form that guided them through a complex process without requiring training they had not received.
Design Decision
Field-level interaction logic throughout: auto-populate from existing complaint data wherever possible; constrained drop-down inputs with manual override; calendar input for all dates; conditional routing if budget or policy related; automated timeline calculation.
Rationale
Every field requiring manual entry is an opportunity for error or avoidance. The form was designed to make the right action the easiest action.
User Need Identified
Complainants needed to participate meaningfully in public hearings, which required advance notice, access to their case file, and a public record of outcomes.
Design Decision
Mandatory SMS notification to complainants at least 48 hours before their case is discussed. Physical copy of ATR provided to the complainant during the hearing. Minutes published online and offline at the panchayat or ward office.
Rationale
A public hearing without informed complainants is a performance, not accountability. Each condition was tested against the question: what does the complainant need in order to show up and challenge the record?
04 — Performance System

Making District Performance Visible

Designing the service was not enough. Without a performance measurement layer there was no structural pressure on district officials to improve. I co-designed a four-indicator composite score used in Chief Minister Window review meetings, ranking all 22 districts in Haryana on a public dashboard. The score was designed so districts could not game a single indicator: all four had to improve simultaneously to move up the ranking. The highest weighting was placed on on-time disposal because timely resolution is the primary purpose of the service.

Responsiveness — 20%
Average Days to Undertake
Scored on a 10-point scale. Districts taking 0 to 4 days score 10. Districts taking 36 to 50 days receive minus 1. Chronic delays score minus 2 or minus 3. Penalty scoring was deliberate: delays should damage performance, not merely fail to improve it.
Quality — 25%
ATR Clarification Ratio
Measures whether officers are filing complete, credible reports. If more clarifications are sought than ATRs sent, score is zero. This prevents high-volume, low-quality filing as a strategy.
Efficiency — 25%
Overdue Complaint Profile
Percentage of overdue complaints in four time bands: under 1 month, 1 to 3 months, 3 to 6 months, over 6 months. Longer-overdue complaints are weighted more heavily.
On-Time Disposal — 30%
Complaints Resolved Within Deadline
Highest weighting. Complaints disposed on time count at full value. Complaints disposed late count at half value. The asymmetry between timely and late disposal is intentional.

The composite score was designed to be legible to a Chief Minister in a two-minute briefing and actionable for a District Collector in a planning meeting. Both users needed the same underlying data at different resolutions. The public ranking created competitive pressure between districts that had not previously existed.

Competitive Accountability Between Districts

The public ranking created performance pressure among District Commissioners that had not previously existed. Districts in the bottom half proactively increased ATR filing rates and reduced overdue complaints before the next review cycle.

Citizen Participation in Public Hearings

Mandatory SMS notification and physical ATR copy requirements changed how complainants experienced the service. Citizens arrived at hearings with their acknowledgement receipts and used portal printouts to directly challenge officers on documented discrepancies.

Service Architecture Adopted in Karnataka

The service blueprint — six-touchpoint model, three-tier escalation structure, and ATR interaction specifications — was adopted as the framework for Karnataka’s integrated grievance redress platform, adapted through similar user-centred co-design processes.
